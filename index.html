Thoughts on Accessibility

If there’s one thing that I’ve learned about accessibility, in general, is that the older I get, the more of a soapbox it becomes. I have a BS in Interior Design, and a significant portion of that coursework revolves around accessibility in building and designing. Then, ten years ago, I got some practical experience with this when my older sister fell down a flight of stairs and broke both of her ankles at the same time; she was in a wheelchair for three months, and she had to live with me because her rent house was completely inaccessible to her.

Being able to navigate the web is as important, in many cases, as being able to navigate one’s physical environment. Due to the amount of tasks that are housed online, the end user must be able to easily use any website, regardless of design. Since we obviously can’t expect a person to change his abilities, we must change the way we build and design websites in order to accommodate them. This means not putting text inside of photos, utilizing special accessibility tools that make buttons keyboard/voice accessible, using readable fonts and subtitles, etc.

We often consider accessibility to be only for those with disabilities, but in reality, accessibility matters to people using smart watches, keyboards, as well as situational issues (such as not having their glasses or being able to listen to audio) or just having slow internet. It is imperative that when we’re designing websites or updating websites, that we take all kinds of end users into account, so that the experience is universally accessible.

Progressive Enhancement

Progressive enhancement is an approach to creating websites that focuses on the content first. Much like the way we have created our projects in class, the HTML is considered first, so that the actual content of the website is available to anyone who seeks after the information that it provides. Then, the CSS and JavaScript are added to allow additional design and functionality/interactivity for those that can access it through their more advanced browser.

It’s important to take this concept into consideration when creating websites for the masses because accessibility is probably the most important aspect of a website’s design. Without the CSS or JavaScript, the website may be boring, but the information is still available to the masses; however, if the content isn’t accessible to all devices and browsers, then there is a portion of the audience that cannot access whatever it is that you’re providing.
